Resources

    Translations
  • (2010) Before writing this translation, Taoist Master Huang was imprisoned during the Cultural Revolution. He says that during his thirteen-year confinement, meditating on the hexagrams gave him the strength to keep going.
  • (1882) A Scottish missionary, James Legge created what is widely regarded as the first translation of the Changes into a Western language that is faithful to the original Chinese text.
  • (1967) The Wilhelm-Baynes translation is perhaps the most influential English rendering of the text. Largely thanks to its foreword by psychoanalyst C.G. Jung, the book became a favorite of the 1960s counterculture.
  • (2004) Richard John Lynn's translation is notable for including the commentary of Wang Bi (226-249 CE). Wang Bi's suggestion that the hexagrams should be analyzed for underlying principles rather than literal meaning has been hugely influential on subsequent interpretations.
  • (2014) In this massive volume, John Minford provides readers with both a translation of the entire Changes with commentaries showing how it's been interpreted, as well as another translation of the core Zhouyi text, with commentaries examining how it was understood in the first centuries of its existence.
    • Books on the I Ching
  • (2014) Geoffrey Redmond and Tze-ki Hon's highly accessible book gives an overview of the meaning and history of the Yijing.
  • (1995) In these lectures, Changes translator Richard Wilhelm and his son Hellmut elucidate the psychological and philosophical understanding of man implicit in the ancient text.
  • (2006) Deng Ming-Dao explores the Yijing's potential as a guide to living. The book includes original translation by Ming-Dao, as well as original prose, poetry and visual art.
  • (1979) In this beautifully illustrated translation, R.L. Wing presents the Changes as a self-help book.
  • (2012) Richard Smith gives a comprehensive overview of the Yijing's 3,000-year history. He describes the evolution of the text in China, how it spread to the rest of Asia, and eventually to the rest of the world.
